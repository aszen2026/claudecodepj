What Happened

On April 28, 2026, Microsoft and OpenAI formally ended the exclusivity clause that had bound OpenAI's frontier models to Azure since 2023. Within 24 hours, Amazon Web Services announced it is already serving new OpenAI products to its customers — a turnaround so fast it suggests AWS had the integration staged and ready for the moment the contract changed.

The same week, Salesforce shipped a new Slackbot AI agent aimed squarely at Microsoft Copilot and Google's Workspace agents. Salesforce is positioning Slack as the conversational front door to enterprise work, with the agent handling summarization, action items, and cross-app retrieval inside the channels where employees already operate.

Read together, these are not two unrelated stories. They are the same story from two angles: the post-exclusivity era of enterprise AI has officially started, and Microsoft's structural advantages from the original 2023 deal are being unwound in real time.

  • April 27: OpenAI secures Microsoft's agreement to drop Azure exclusivity on new products.
  • April 28: AWS lists new OpenAI offerings in its catalog — same-day distribution.
  • April 28–29: Salesforce announces Slackbot AI agent, framing the launch explicitly as a workplace-AI fight with Microsoft and Google.

Why It Matters

For three years, Microsoft's lock on OpenAI's flagship models was the single most valuable distribution moat in enterprise software. That moat is now porous. The immediate winners and losers:

  • OpenAI wins distribution leverage. It can now negotiate compute and revenue-share with AWS, Google Cloud, and Oracle on equal footing, which materially changes its margin structure and reduces single-vendor risk.
  • AWS wins the most underpriced asset of the week. Customers who wanted OpenAI models but were locked into AWS-centric data, IAM, and VPC topology no longer have to bridge clouds. The same-day rollout suggests Bedrock-style hosting was pre-built.
  • Microsoft loses optionality, not revenue — yet. Azure OpenAI Service remains massive, and Microsoft retains its equity position. But the scarcity premium Azure charged for being the only place to run GPT-class models is gone.
  • Salesforce sees an opening. If Copilot is no longer the only credible enterprise AI assistant tied to a frontier model, Slack-as-agent-surface becomes a defensible play rather than a flanking move.
  • Google sits in the middle. Gemini-in-Workspace was already competitive on price; a multi-cloud OpenAI lowers the differentiation of Microsoft's bundle, which indirectly helps Google's pitch on Workspace.
💡
The contract change matters more than any model release. A new model shifts benchmarks for a quarter. A distribution change reshapes which clouds, which sales motions, and which procurement contracts win for years.

Context

The 2023 Microsoft–OpenAI agreement gave Azure exclusive rights to host OpenAI's API and embedded GPT-4-class models into Microsoft 365 Copilot, GitHub Copilot, and Bing. That deal was the template every other hyperscaler tried to copy: Anthropic with AWS, Mistral with Azure (ironically), Cohere with Oracle.

What's happening now is the unwinding of that template. Three trends converge:

  • Frontier-model commoditization. With GPT-5.x, Claude 4.x, and Gemini 2.5-class models all clustered near the same capability frontier, exclusivity has less product value than it did in the GPT-4 era.
  • Regulatory pressure. The FTC and EU competition authorities have been examining cloud–model exclusivity since 2024. Voluntarily ending exclusivity is cheaper than litigating it.
  • OpenAI's compute appetite. Reaching the next training run requires more capacity than any single cloud can responsibly commit. Multi-cloud is now an engineering requirement, not just a commercial preference.

The Salesforce move fits the same pattern from the application layer: when the model layer gets unbundled from the cloud, the workflow surface — the inbox, the channel, the CRM record — becomes the durable point of differentiation.

What To Do Next

If you're a developer building on OpenAI: revisit your provider abstraction. The constraint that pushed teams to wrap OpenAI calls behind a thin interface — "in case we need to switch" — has now become a constraint to wrap them behind a cloud interface. Same models, different IAM, different egress, different compliance posture.

If you're a buyer comparing Copilot and Slackbot: the question is no longer "whose model is better." It's "where does work actually happen in our company?" If your default chat surface is Teams, Copilot's integration tax is lower. If it's Slack, Salesforce's new agent just got materially more credible.

If you're on a cloud procurement team: renegotiate. Any AI-related Azure commit signed in the last 18 months was priced against an exclusivity premium that no longer exists. AWS and Google will use OpenAI availability as a wedge through Q3.

💡
Action this week: audit which of your AI workloads are on Azure specifically because OpenAI was only on Azure. Those are now portable — and that portability is leverage you can spend in your next renewal conversation.

The exclusivity era ended quietly, in a contract amendment rather than a keynote. But the speed of AWS's response — and the timing of Salesforce's launch — show how much pent-up competitive energy was waiting for it. Expect Google Cloud's OpenAI announcement next, and expect Microsoft to respond not by clawing back exclusivity, but by leaning harder into the one place it still has a structural lock: the Office surface where 400 million knowledge workers already live.
