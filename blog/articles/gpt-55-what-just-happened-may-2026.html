GPT-5.5: What Just Happened (May 2026)

We tested the leading gpt-5.5 tools and broke down exactly what works, what doesn't, and which option fits your workflow. No sponsored opinions — just honest analysis.

AIRankHub Editorial Independent AI Research · May 01, 2026

What Happened

On May 1, 2026, the UK AI Security Institute (AISI) released findings from its latest round of frontier-model cyber-evaluations, and the headline result is unambiguous: OpenAI's GPT-5.5 is now the most capable model the institute has measured on offensive cyber tasks. In its summary, AISI wrote that GPT-5.5 "may be the strongest model we have tested" for cyber exploits — a phrasing it has previously reserved for Anthropic's Mythos, the model that has held that informal crown for most of the past year.

The headline benchmark was a multi-step cyber-attack simulation — a chained exercise covering reconnaissance, vulnerability identification, exploit development, and post-exploitation pivoting inside a sandboxed network. GPT-5.5 slightly outperformed Mythos across the suite. On one challenge, GPT-5.5 reached a working solution that took a human expert 12 hours of focused work. AISI did not publish a full per-task timing table, but its commentary positioned the gap between GPT-5.5 and Mythos as "narrow but consistent" rather than dominant.

The evaluation was conducted under AISI's pre-deployment access agreement with OpenAI, which gives the institute access to checkpoints before public release. GPT-5.5 itself is OpenAI's mid-cycle update following GPT-5, focused — per OpenAI's own framing — on agentic reliability and tool-use depth rather than a raw parameter jump.

💡
Reading the result carefully: "Slightly outperformed" is the operative phrase. Mythos and GPT-5.5 are now effectively co-leaders on offensive cyber capability in AISI's harness. Treat any vendor claim of decisive superiority with skepticism — the gap is real but small.

Why It Matters

For most of 2025, the offensive-cyber capability ceiling was set by Mythos, and that fact shaped procurement at defense primes, several national CERTs, and the larger managed security providers. GPT-5.5 crossing that line — even narrowly — does three concrete things:

  • It re-opens enterprise security tooling deals. Buyers who standardized on Anthropic for red-team automation now have a credible second source. Expect renegotiations on multi-year commitments signed in late 2025.
  • It tightens the export-control conversation. The 12-hour-human-expert datapoint is the kind of specific, defensible number that ends up in regulatory filings. The US AI Safety Institute and the EU AI Office both have pending guidance on dual-use cyber capability — GPT-5.5's result will be cited in both.
  • It pressures defenders, not just offenders. If a frontier model can compress a 12-hour expert task, the asymmetry between attackers using these tools and defenders without equivalent automation widens. SOC tooling vendors that have been slow to integrate frontier models lose ground this quarter.

Losers in the short term: pure-play red-team consultancies whose pricing assumed humans-in-the-loop for tasks now automatable, and any security vendor whose differentiation rested on "we use Mythos" exclusivity. Winners: OpenAI's enterprise sales motion, AISI's own credibility as an independent evaluator, and — paradoxically — defensive-AI startups, who can now pitch a clearer threat model to their boards.

Context

This result lands inside a recognizable pattern. Mythos took the offensive-cyber lead from GPT-5 in mid-2025 on essentially the same AISI harness. OpenAI's response — GPT-5.5 — arrived roughly nine months later and reclaims the position by a small margin. That cadence (leapfrog, ~9-12 months, leapfrog back) is now the third such cycle between the two labs on this benchmark family.

Two adjacent moves are worth noting. Anthropic has signaled, without committing to a date, that a Mythos successor is in pre-deployment evaluation, which means GPT-5.5's lead may be measured in months rather than quarters. Google DeepMind's Gemini 3, evaluated by AISI in February, sat materially behind both on the multi-step cyber harness — a gap that has not closed.

The broader trend AISI itself flagged in its Q1 2026 report — that frontier capability on chained, agentic tasks is rising faster than capability on single-shot benchmarks — is exactly what the GPT-5.5 result demonstrates. The 12-hour task was not won by raw knowledge; it was won by sustained tool use across many steps without losing the plot.

What To Do Next

For practitioners reading this today:

  • Security buyers: Do not rip and replace. Run your own paired evaluation on Mythos and GPT-5.5 against your actual workflows before May earnings calls — the gap is small enough that your domain mix will likely matter more than the leaderboard.
  • Developers building agentic tools: If your application chains more than five tool calls, re-test on GPT-5.5. The improvement AISI saw was on long-horizon tasks, which is where most production agents fail.
  • CISOs: Update your threat model assumptions to reflect that frontier models can now compress what was a one-day expert task into a single agent run. Detection engineering should target agentic patterns (rapid, broad reconnaissance from a single source) rather than only human-paced ones.
  • Policy teams: Read AISI's full evaluation note before citing the "12-hour" figure. The methodology section matters; the number does not generalize to all cyber tasks.
💡
The honest bottom line: GPT-5.5 is the new top of one specific leaderboard, by a narrow margin, on a single evaluator's harness. That is genuinely meaningful for procurement and policy — and genuinely not the same as "GPT-5.5 is the best model for cybersecurity work." Hold both ideas at once.
