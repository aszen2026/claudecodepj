Openai IPO: What Just Happened (May 2026)

What Happened

OpenAI is officially in IPO-exploration territory. Greg Brockman, OpenAI's co-founder and president, confirmed under oath during Elon Musk's ongoing lawsuit against the company that OpenAI is "exploring" a public offering — the first time a senior executive has made that statement on the record in a legal setting.

The disclosure came as testimony in the Musk v. OpenAI trial, where Musk has argued that OpenAI's pivot away from its nonprofit roots constitutes a breach of its founding mission. Brockman's comment wasn't a formal announcement — no ticker symbol, no S-1 filing, no investment bank lineup has been named — but the word "exploring" from a co-founder under oath carries considerably more weight than a leaked memo or an anonymous source.

Meanwhile, OpenAI's hardware partner Cerebras Systems is pressing forward with what analysts are calling a potential blockbuster IPO of its own. Cerebras, which builds the wafer-scale CS-3 AI accelerator chip and has a deep commercial relationship with OpenAI, is reported to be on track for a public listing that could value the company in the tens of billions of dollars.

💡
Key detail: Brockman's IPO comment came under oath in federal court, not in a press release. That distinction matters — executives face legal consequences for materially false statements in that setting, lending the disclosure unusual credibility.

Why It Matters

An OpenAI IPO would be one of the largest technology listings in history. The company was last privately valued at $157 billion following its October 2023 funding round, and subsequent secondary market transactions have pushed implied valuations even higher. A public offering would force OpenAI to open its books — revenue, burn rate, gross margins on ChatGPT, API pricing economics — to a level of scrutiny it has never faced as a private company.

For the broader AI industry, the implications split along clear lines:

  • Developers and enterprise buyers would gain far more financial transparency into OpenAI's sustainability as a platform dependency. Right now, no one outside the company knows how long its current pricing model is subsidized by Microsoft's $13 billion investment.
  • Competitors — Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta AI, xAI — would face intensified investor pressure to articulate their own monetization paths or follow suit with their own public market events.
  • Microsoft, which holds a significant revenue-share arrangement with OpenAI, could see its stake structure scrutinized and potentially repriced as public market mechanics take over from private round valuations.
  • Elon Musk's legal case gains a new dimension: an IPO would cement OpenAI's for-profit transition, potentially strengthening some of Musk's core arguments about mission drift even as it complicates the legal remedy he's seeking.

The Cerebras IPO adds a second data point. If Cerebras — a company whose primary distinguishing relationship is its proximity to OpenAI's infrastructure needs — can command a blockbuster valuation, it signals that public markets are ready to absorb AI infrastructure risk at scale.

Context

OpenAI completed its conversion from a nonprofit to a Public Benefit Corporation structure in early 2025, a structural prerequisite for any conventional IPO. That restructuring drew immediate legal fire from Musk, several state attorneys general, and former employees. The Brockman testimony is the first time the IPO topic has surfaced in that litigation context, suggesting OpenAI's legal team considers the possibility defensible — or unavoidable.

The timing also follows a brutal year for AI startup funding. While OpenAI and Anthropic continued to raise mega-rounds, the broader seed and Series A market for AI companies contracted sharply through late 2025 as investors demanded clearer paths to profitability. A successful OpenAI IPO would reopen institutional capital flows to the sector in a way that private rounds cannot.

Cerebras's trajectory is instructive. The company filed an IPO prospectus in 2024, had it paused amid regulatory review of its ties to UAE-based G42, then resumed the process. Its chip, the CS-3, competes directly with Nvidia's H100 and H200 GPUs on specific large-model training and inference workloads. If its listing succeeds, it will be the first pure-play AI chip company to go public since Nvidia's own era-defining run — and a direct test of whether investors will pay a premium for non-Nvidia silicon.

💡
Watch this relationship: OpenAI uses Cerebras hardware for specific inference workloads. If Cerebras goes public before OpenAI, its S-1 disclosures may reveal more about OpenAI's actual infrastructure economics than OpenAI itself has ever published.

What To Do Next

If you build on the OpenAI API, the single most important action is to review your vendor concentration risk. An IPO process typically lasts 12–18 months from exploration to first trade. During that window, pricing structures, rate limits, and enterprise contract terms may shift as OpenAI aligns its commercial model with public market expectations. Audit your integration now and identify which workloads could move to Anthropic's Claude API, Google's Gemini API, or open-weight models like Meta's Llama series if pricing changes materially.

For enterprise procurement teams negotiating OpenAI contracts in the next six months:

  • Push for multi-year pricing locks before any S-1 is filed — post-filing, commercial terms typically freeze or harden.
  • Ask vendors for their own contingency plans if OpenAI API pricing increases post-IPO.
  • Evaluate whether Cerebras's CS-3 accelerators, once the company is publicly accountable, become a credible alternative for on-premises inference at scale.

For investors watching the AI infrastructure space: the Cerebras IPO will arrive first and should be read as a leading indicator of how public markets price AI-infrastructure companies with concentrated customer relationships. Its reception will almost certainly inform OpenAI's own timing and valuation targets. Watch the Cerebras roadshow closely — it may be the best preview of the OpenAI prospectus we get before the thing is filed.
