Google Launch: What Just Happened (April 2026)

What Happened

On April 30, 2026, Google rolled out Deep Research Max, an autonomous research agent positioned as a tier above the existing Deep Research feature inside Gemini. Unlike the earlier version, which produced a structured report from a guided prompt in a few minutes, Deep Research Max runs as a long-horizon agent: it plans its own sub-questions, reads across the open web, and returns what Google describes as an "expert-grade" written report without further user prompting.

The launch did not arrive alone. The same news cycle includes two adjacent stories that frame what Google is doing:

  • Salesforce shipped a new Slackbot AI agent, explicitly framed as a workplace-AI counterpunch against Microsoft Copilot and Google's Workspace agents.
  • Google Photos launched an AI try-on feature that lets users virtually try on clothes they already own from their personal photo library.

Read together, the three releases sketch Google's posture for late April 2026: ship one flagship agent for knowledge work (Deep Research Max), one consumer-AI feature to keep Photos sticky (try-on), and let competitors confirm the category by chasing it (Salesforce in Slack).

Why It Matters

Deep Research Max moves Google's research product from "assistant" to "agent" in the practical sense buyers care about: the user defines the question, the model does the multi-hour grind. That has direct revenue consequences for three groups.

Consulting and equity-research juniors are the most exposed. The bulk of a first-year analyst's output — comparable-company write-ups, market sizings, regulatory landscape memos — is exactly what an autonomous research agent is built to produce. A Deep Research Max report is not a board deck, but it is a credible first draft that previously consumed days of billable time.

OpenAI and Anthropic now face a Gemini feature that is genuinely differentiated rather than catch-up. ChatGPT's Deep Research and Claude's research mode are competent, but Google's distribution through Workspace and Search means Deep Research Max lands in front of hundreds of millions of users without an install step.

Salesforce and Microsoft are competing in an adjacent lane. The new Slackbot agent is about workflow inside the team's existing chat surface — answering "what did we decide about pricing?" — not producing a 30-page market report. Both bets can win, but they are not the same product, and conflating them in procurement is going to cost some buyers money this year.

💡
Buyer's note: Deep Research Max competes with research vendors (analyst seats, market-intel subscriptions). Slackbot AI competes with internal-knowledge tools. If your team is evaluating both, separate the budgets and the success metrics — otherwise you will under-buy one and over-buy the other.

Context

Deep Research Max is the logical next step in a trajectory Google has been on for more than a year. The original Deep Research shipped in Gemini in late 2024 as a guided multi-step research flow. Through 2025, Google upgraded it onto successive Gemini models, lengthened the context window, and let it cite more sources. "Max" is the version that drops the training wheels: longer runs, more autonomous planning, fewer user interventions.

The competitive context is equally clear. OpenAI introduced its own Deep Research mode in early 2025, and Anthropic followed with Claude's research capability. Perplexity has been pushing agentic research as its core wedge. Google's advantage is not novelty — it is that Gemini already sits inside Gmail, Docs, and Workspace, where the research output needs to land.

The Google Photos try-on launch fits a separate but parallel trend: virtual try-on has been a consumer-AI battleground since Google Shopping introduced generative try-on for apparel in 2023. Extending it to clothes the user already owns turns Photos from a passive archive into an active styling tool, and it puts pressure on standalone wardrobe apps and on Pinterest's try-on features.

Salesforce's Slackbot move, meanwhile, is a defensive play. Microsoft has been embedding Copilot agents into Teams aggressively through 2025 and 2026, and Google's Workspace agents have been picking up share in mid-market. A native Slack agent is the minimum table-stakes response.

What To Do Next

Concrete actions, depending on who you are:

  • If you are a Workspace customer: Check whether Deep Research Max is included in your existing Gemini for Workspace tier or gated behind a higher SKU. Google has a habit of launching premium agents on the top plan first.
  • If you run a research function: Have one analyst run the same brief through Deep Research Max, ChatGPT Deep Research, and Claude this week. The differences in citation quality, hallucination rate, and report structure are the only data that matters for your tooling decision.
  • If you are evaluating Slack vs. Teams vs. Workspace AI: Do not pick on the agent demo. Pick on which surface your team actually lives in. The best agent in the wrong app gets ignored.
  • If you build on the Gemini API: Watch for whether Google exposes Deep Research Max as a developer-callable endpoint. The original Deep Research was UI-only for months; if Max follows the same pattern, third-party research products have a window before the API ships.
  • If you ship a consumer app with a photo library: Google Photos try-on raises the floor on what users expect from any app that holds their images. Audit whether your retention story still holds when Photos can do styling natively.
💡
One thing to test today: Run Deep Research Max on a topic you already know cold. The fastest way to calibrate any agent's reliability is to grade it on ground truth you own — not on a topic where the polished output looks impressive because you cannot check it.
